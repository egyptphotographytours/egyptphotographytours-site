What to Eat? Must-Try Dishes from Alexandria and Beyond

Iconic Egyptian Dishes You Don't Want to Miss in Alexandria

When pondering what to eat in Alexandria, Egypt, be sure to try iconic dishes that reflect the city's rich culinary heritage. These dishes capture the essence of Egyptian culture and the unique flavors of Alexandria's culinary scene.

Koshary

Koshary is a hearty and inexpensive vegan dish from Egypt, celebrated for its satisfying combination of rice, lentils, pasta, and chickpeas, making it a nutritious choice. The base consists of fluffy rice mixed with protein-rich lentils and small pasta shapes, creating a delightful medley of textures. To elevate the dish, it's generously topped with a spiced tomato sauce, often infused with garlic, cumin, and vinegar, which adds a zesty flavor profile. Crispy fried onions are commonly sprinkled on top for an added crunch and depth of taste. Koshary is a popular street food and a staple lunch option, loved for its affordability and ability to fill you up while catering to various dietary preferences. This iconic dish not only reflects the rich culinary heritage of Egypt but also exemplifies the resourcefulness in creating a delicious, satisfying meal from simple ingredients.

Fattah

Fattah is a traditional and festive Egyptian dish typically enjoyed during special occasions and religious holidays, renowned for its rich and comforting flavors. This elaborate meal features layers of tender, savory beef alongside rice and crispy fried bread, creating a delightful combination of textures and tastes. The dish is generously topped with a flavorful red sauce made from garlic, tomatoes, and an array of spices, enhancing the meal's overall depth and richness. Often served during significant celebrations like Eid and weddings, Fattah is considered a symbol of festivity and abundance, making it a cherished culinary staple. While it serves as a satisfying lunch dish, the preparation and ingredients make it quite expensive compared to everyday meals. Its celebratory nature and intricate layers make Fattah a centerpiece at gatherings, bringing families and friends together to share in the joy of communal dining. The rich flavors and deep-rooted tradition make Fattah a beloved dish, emboding Egypt's culinary heritage and the spirit of togetherness during special moments.

Mahshi

Mahshi is a cherished Egyptian dish featuring stuffed vegetables, typically zucchini or eggplant, filled with a savory mixture of rice, herbs, and spices. In some variations, rice is combined with tomato sauce, spices, and fresh herbs like parsley or dill, and the filling can also be wrapped in grape leaves or white cabbage. The stuffed vegetables or wrapped leaves are then cooked in chicken stock, infusing them with rich flavor. Mahshi is often served as a lunch course alongside Molokhia soup or lisan al asfour (bird tongue soup), complemented by fried poultry, creating a satisfying and hearty meal. This dish is a cult favorite in Egyptian households, especially on Fridays, which is considered a day off from work and a time for families to gather and enjoy traditional meals together. Mahshi's delightful flavors and communal aspect make it an integral part of Egyptian culinary culture.

Unique Desserts in Alexandria: Sweet Treats You Need to Try

When exploring what to eat in Alexandria, Egypt, don't miss the unique traditional desserts that define the local sweet scene. These desserts highlight Egyptian cuisine's diverse and rich flavors, making them must-try treats in Alexandria.

Basbousa

Basbousa is a tasty sweet, semolina cake popular across Egypt and the Middle East. Made from a mixture of semolina, sugar, yogurt, and baking powder, it has a dense yet moist texture. Once baked, Basbousa is generously soaked in a fragrant syrup made from sugar and water, usually flavored with a hint of lemon or rose water, which adds a delightful sweetness and aromatic quality. Often garnished with blanched almonds or shredded coconut, this dessert is enjoyed on special occasions and festivals, symbolizing hospitality and celebration in Egyptian culture. Its simplicity and rich flavor make Basbousa a cherished treat, that is enjoyed by many. It easily found in homes, restaurants, and sweet shops throughout Egypt.

Knafeh (or Kunafa)

Knafeh is a luscious dessert made from thin, noodle-like dough called kataifi, traditionally filled with sweet cheese. Once baked to a golden crisp, it is generously topped with syrup, creating a rich and satisfying experience for those who indulge. This dessert is particularly popular during Ramadan, where it is often enjoyed as a sweet treat after the main meal, typically served with black tea to complement its flavors. During this special month, many variations of Knafeh are available, including enticing options filled with mango, cream, whipped cream, or nuts, catering to a wide range of tastes. Knafeh is commonly soaked in honey or sugar water in more confectioneries, enhancing its sweetness and appeal. This decadent dessert not only symbolizes the joy of Ramadan but also brings families and friends together to share in the delight of these cherished flavors.

Umm Ali (or Om Ali)

Umm Ali is a classic Egyptian dessert that embodies comfort and tradition, often enjoyed as a warm bread pudding. This delightful dish is made with layers of flaky pastry, which are torn into pieces and combined with milk, sugar, and a variety of nuts such as almonds and pistachios. Baked until golden and bubbling, Umm Ali is characterized by its rich, creamy texture and sweet flavor, making it a favorite for gatherings and special occasions. Served warm, this indulgent dessert not only satisfies the sweet tooth but also showcases the warmth of Egyptian hospitality, as it is often shared among family and friends. With its delightful combination of ingredients, Umm Ali holds a special place in Egyptian cuisine, representing the country's rich culinary heritage and love for comforting sweets.

Qatayef (or Atayef)

Qatayef are delightful stuffed mini pancakes that are especially popular during Ramadan, offering a sweet treat after breaking the fast. These pancakes are made from a simple batter and cooked until fluffy and tender, then filled with various of fillings, most commonly nuts or sweet cream. After being generously stuffed, Qatayef are often folded into half-moons and can be either fried for a crispy texture or left unbaked for a softer bite. Drizzled with syrup or served with a dusting of powdered sugar, Qatayef are a favorite among families and friends during the holy month, symbolizing the joy of celebration and togetherness. Their versatility and indulgent flavors make them a cherished dessert, embodying the spirit of Ramadan in Egyptian culture.

Roz Bil Laban

Rice pudding, or Roz Bil Laban, is a beloved dessert across the Arab world, valued for its cold, rich, and creamy texture. It is a perfect choice for cooling off during the summer. Made by heating rice and milk on the stovetop, it's quick and easy to prepare for any family gathering. Numerous ice cream parlors serve this iconic dessert along Alexandria's Corniche, the city's main street overlooking the Mediterranean Sea. Here are three recommended places to enjoy the best Roz Bil Laban in Alexandria:

Gelaty Makram
Established in the 1940s, this family-run ice cream parlor has hosted notable figures like Anwar El Sadat and Hosni Mubarak. It's a beloved spot for locals, and we recommend trying their signature rice pudding topped with mixed nuts.

Saber Patisserie
Starting in 1960 as a small cart, this shop has expanded to over 20 branches in Egypt. Known for pioneering winter ice cream sales. We recommend visiting the Mostafa Kamel branch and sampling their Roz Bil Laban with mixed nuts.

Ice Cream Azza
Often hailed Alexandria's most famous dessert shop, Azza began as a cart in the 1960s and was founded by Muhammed Jamal. Popular for its ice cream, especially milk and mango flavors. We recommend enjoying their Roz Bil Laban with mixed nuts at the Bahari branch overlooking the sea.

These spots embody Alexandria's rich dessert culture, making Roz Bil Laban a visitor must-try.
