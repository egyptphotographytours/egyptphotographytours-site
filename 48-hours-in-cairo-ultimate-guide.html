48 Hours in Cairo: The Ultimate Itinerary

A hour-by-hour Cairo travel guide for photographers, first-time visitors, and anyone with just two days to experience the Pyramids, ancient temples, and timeless streets of Egypt's capital.

Home/ Blog/ 48 Hours in Cairo
Updated regularly 22 min read Cairo & Giza, Egypt By Hossam, Egyptologist & Photographer

Cairo is a city of more than 20 million people where ancient wonders sit a short drive from modern traffic, where the call to prayer drifts over rooftop cafés, and where every street seems to hold a story centuries deep. For travelers with just two days — a long weekend, a stopover between flights, or a quick add-on to a longer Egypt itinerary — the challenge is simple to state and hard to solve: how do you experience the real Cairo without losing the entire visit to traffic, queues, and guesswork?

This guide is built to solve exactly that. It isn't a generic checklist — it's a tested, hour-by-hour framework developed from years of guiding photographers and first-time visitors through Cairo's most rewarding sites. Whether you book one of our guided Egypt tours or explore independently using this guide, you'll find a complete itinerary, transportation logistics, cultural etiquette, a realistic budget, and the photography techniques that turn ordinary snapshots into images worth keeping.

Before You Go: Essential Cairo Intelligence

A great 48 hours in Cairo starts before you ever land. This city rewards preparation — and a little planning up front means you'll spend your limited time exploring instead of figuring things out as you go.

When to Visit: Timing Your Trip

Cairo's climate shifts dramatically across the year, and the season you choose shapes everything from comfort to crowd levels. Unlike many destinations, Cairo doesn't have an "off-season" in the sense of closures — it's a genuinely year-round city. For a deeper seasonal breakdown across the whole country, see our full Egypt weather guide.

Cairo seasons at a glance
SeasonMonthsTypical TempsCrowdsBest For
Peak SeasonDec – Feb48–66°F (9–19°C)Very HighComfortable sightseeing, book early
Shoulder SeasonMar–May, Sep–Nov66–90°F (19–32°C)ModeratePhotography & comfort balance

Insider tip: Whatever the season, the first hour after sunrise and the last hour before sunset deliver the softest light and the smallest crowds at the Pyramids. Build your schedule around those windows and the rest of the day falls into place.

Cultural Etiquette & Local Customs

Respectful Travel in Cairo

  • Dress modestly: Cover shoulders and knees, especially when visiting mosques or markets.
  • Tipping (baksheesh): Small tips ($1–$5) are customary for minor services and guidance.
  • Photography permissions: Tripods are restricted in many locations and drones require government clearance — see our full photography rules section below.
  • Friday prayers: Many sites adjust hours around midday Friday prayers (roughly 11:30 AM–1 PM).
  • Bargaining: Expected at markets like Khan el-Khalili — starting offers are usually negotiable.

What to Pack

Beyond the obvious (passport, chargers, comfortable shoes) a few Cairo-specific items make a real difference:

  • Photography: A versatile zoom lens (24–70mm equivalent), polarizing filter, spare batteries, and a lens-cleaning cloth — desert dust is constant.
  • Clothing: Lightweight, breathable layers that cover shoulders and knees; a light scarf for mosque visits.
  • Cash: Egyptian Pounds (EGP) for tips, markets, and small vendors — cards are increasingly common but cash still smooths daily transactions.
  • Sun protection: High-SPF sunscreen, a hat, and sunglasses, even in cooler months.
  • Power bank: Long sightseeing days drain phones fast, especially with GPS and photos running constantly.

The Full 48-Hour Cairo Itinerary

This is the heart of the guide: a complete, hour-by-hour plan built around light, logistics, and experience flow. Use it exactly as written, or treat it as a flexible framework and adapt the pacing to your group.

Day 1 — Giza Pyramids, Grand Egyptian Museum & Islamic Cairo

~5 miles of walking
7:00 AM — Sunrise at the Pyramids of Giza

Arrive as the gates open. Most tour buses arrive between 9–10 AM, so the first hour belongs almost entirely to early risers. If you want to go inside the Great Pyramid, buy that ticket immediately — daily entry slots are limited. For deeper context on what's actually inside, see our guide: What's Really Inside the Pyramids of Giza?

7:30 – 10:30 AM — Giza Plateau Exploration

Sequence: Great Pyramid exterior → Panoramic Point → Pyramid of Khafre → the Great Sphinx. The Panoramic Point delivers the classic three-pyramid skyline shot; save detail shots of the limestone blocks for when the light is still raking and golden.

10:30 – 11:30 AM — Breakfast with Pyramid Views

A handful of restaurants near the plateau serve breakfast with direct pyramid views — reserve ahead and arrange return transport in advance, since flagging a taxi independently from these spots can be difficult.

11:30 AM – 3:30 PM — Grand Egyptian Museum

Budget three to four unhurried hours. The GEM houses over 100,000 artifacts, including the complete Tutankhamun collection, and is genuinely one of the great museums in the world. Multiple on-site cafés make it easy to combine the visit with lunch.

3:30 – 5:00 PM — Refresh & Recharge

Return to your hotel, back up photos, charge batteries, and change into comfortable shoes for an evening of walking through Islamic Cairo.

5:00 – 7:00 PM — Islamic Cairo & Al-Muizz Street

Start at Bab Zuweila and walk north along Al-Muizz Li-Din Allah Street — one of the densest concentrations of medieval Islamic architecture anywhere in the world. Golden-hour light on the limestone facades is some of the best architectural photography Cairo offers.

7:00 – 8:30 PM — Khan el-Khalili Bazaar

Go for the atmosphere as much as the shopping: lanterns, spices, copperware, and centuries of trading history. Always ask permission before photographing inside someone's shop, and expect — and enjoy — friendly bargaining.

8:30 – 10:00 PM — Dinner in the Old City

Choose between an authentic, adventurous local meal or a tourist-friendly rooftop restaurant overlooking the bazaar — both deliver a memorable end to day one.

Day 2 — Saqqara, Dahshur, Memphis & Coptic Cairo

~4 miles of walking
7:30 AM — Depart for Saqqara

About a one-hour drive from central Cairo or Giza. Saqqara sees a fraction of Giza's crowds, making it a noticeably calmer, more intimate experience.

8:30 – 10:30 AM — Saqqara Necropolis

Home to the Step Pyramid of Djoser, Egypt's oldest stone pyramid, plus several tombs with exquisitely preserved relief carvings. A licensed guide adds enormous value here — for the broader debate on guided versus independent touring, read Tour Guide or Solo? Exploring Egypt the Best Way.

10:30 – 11:30 AM — Dahshur: Bent & Red Pyramids

A short 15-minute drive from Saqqara. The Bent Pyramid's unusual silhouette and the Red Pyramid's interior chambers make this one of Cairo's most underrated stops.

12:30 – 1:30 PM — Memphis Open-Air Museum

A quick but worthwhile stop at the site of Egypt's ancient capital, anchored by a colossal fallen statue of Ramses II.

1:30 – 3:30 PM — Coptic Cairo

A peaceful, concentrated historic district featuring the Hanging Church, the Church of St. Sergius, and the Ben Ezra Synagogue — a striking contrast to the energy of Islamic Cairo the day before.

3:30 – 4:30 PM — Koshary Break

Egypt's beloved national dish — a hearty mix of rice, lentils, pasta and crispy onions — at an affordable, authentic local spot.

4:30 – 6:00 PM — Museum Choice

Optional second museum visit: the National Museum of Egyptian Civilization for royal mummies in a modern setting, or the historic Egyptian Museum at Tahrir Square for sheer artifact volume. Alternatively, unwind with a felucca sail on the Nile.

7:00 – 8:30 PM — Farewell Dinner

Round out your two days with Egyptian cuisine at a Zamalek restaurant overlooking the Nile, then close the evening with traditional tea or coffee at a local ahwa (coffeehouse) — where most Cairenes actually spend their evenings.

Alternative: A Hidden-Gems Day for Return Visitors

If this isn't your first trip to Cairo, swap either day above for this off-the-beaten-path alternative:

Hidden Cairo

Off the Beaten Path
  • St. Simon Monastery (Cave Church): A remarkable cave church carved into the cliffs of Manshiyat Naser, holding up to 20,000 worshippers.
  • Mosque of Ibn Tulun: One of the oldest mosques in Africa, famous for the way morning light spills through its arches.
  • Gayer-Anderson Museum: A beautifully preserved 17th-century residence beside Ibn Tulun, with a rooftop made famous on film.
  • City of the Dead (Al Qarafa): A UNESCO-listed necropolis where Mamluk-era architecture exists alongside everyday life.

Photography Rules in Egypt You Need to Know

Stay Within the Rules

  • Drones: Illegal without explicit government clearance — don't bring one expecting to fly it casually.
  • Tripods: Restricted at many sites; small monopods are sometimes tolerated.
  • Military & security sites: Never photograph military personnel, installations, or bridges.
  • Museums: Rules vary by venue and sometimes by gallery — always check before shooting.
  • Mosques: Be respectful of prayer times; some restrict photography during services.

Safety & Health Considerations

Staying Healthy

  • Water: Stick to bottled water, including for brushing teeth.
  • Food: Eat at busy, popular spots; freshly cooked hot food is generally safe.
  • Sun & heat: Hydrate often and schedule indoor visits (museums) during peak midday heat.
  • Travel insurance: Always recommended, ideally with medical evacuation coverage.

General Safety

  • Tourist police are present at all major sites and can assist with most issues.
  • Common scams involve unofficial "guides," persistent vendors, and overcharging — agree on prices upfront.
  • Visa requirements vary by nationality — our complete Egyptian visa guide covers every option.
  • Staying connected: A local SIM makes navigation and communication far easier — see our guide to the best SIM card options in Egypt.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 48 hours really enough time to see Cairo?
Yes. With a planned itinerary, two full days are enough to experience Cairo's essential highlights: the Pyramids of Giza and Sphinx, the Grand Egyptian Museum, Islamic Cairo, Khan el-Khalili Bazaar, and either Saqqara or Coptic Cairo. The key is starting early and sequencing sites by location to minimize transit time.
What is the best time of year to visit Cairo?
October through April offers the most comfortable sightseeing temperatures, with March–May and September–November widely considered ideal for photography. Summer is hot but workable with early-morning touring. Cairo is genuinely a year-round destination — see our full weather guide for more detail.
How much does a two-day trip to Cairo cost?
Budget travelers can cover accommodation, food, local transport and entry fees for roughly $115–$200 over 48 hours. Mid-range trips with a private driver run $260–$550, while a fully guided, photography-focused private tour can run $880–$1,800+. International flights are excluded from these estimates.
Do I need a private tour guide for the Pyramids of Giza?
It isn't legally required, but it's highly recommended for a short visit. A licensed Egyptologist guide saves time, explains the history and engineering of the site, and helps you avoid scams from unofficial guides at the entrance — see our full comparison in Tour Guide or Solo?
What should I wear when visiting Cairo, including mosques?
Dress modestly — shoulders and knees covered for everyone. Women should bring a scarf for mosque visits, and shoes come off before entering prayer areas. Comfortable, closed-toe walking shoes are essential given the amount of walking involved.
Is Cairo safe for tourists?
Cairo is generally safe for tourists who take normal precautions. Tourist police are stationed at major sites, and the more common issues are overcharging and persistent vendors rather than serious crime. Booking through an established operator significantly reduces hassle.
Can I take photos at the Pyramids and inside museums?
General photography for personal use is allowed at most outdoor sites including the Pyramids and Saqqara. Drones require government permission and are effectively off-limits casually, tripods are restricted in many locations, and museum photography rules vary by venue — see our photography rules section above.
What's the best way to get around Cairo on a short trip?
Ride-hailing apps (Uber and Careem) work well for independent travelers, while a private driver or guided tour is more efficient across multiple sites in one day. Self-driving isn't recommended given local traffic conditions.
Do I need a visa to visit Egypt?
Most nationalities can obtain a visa on arrival or apply for an e-Visa online in advance. Requirements vary by passport — our complete visa guide walks through every option step by step.
Should I book a private tour or join a group tour in Cairo?
For a tight 48-hour schedule, a private tour is usually the better choice because the itinerary can be built entirely around your pace. Group tours can be more budget-friendly if a fixed schedule works for you — we compare both in detail in Tour Guide or Solo? Exploring Egypt the Best Way.

Final Thoughts: Making the Most of Your Time in Cairo

Cairo rewards travelers who plan but doesn't punish those who wander. Your 48 hours here will likely feel both impossibly short and remarkably full — a blend of ancient stone, modern street life, and small unplanned moments that often end up being the most memorable part of the trip.

Whether you follow this itinerary precisely or use it as a flexible starting point, whether you explore independently or with one of our guides, two days in Cairo is enough to understand why so many travelers return to Egypt again and again.
