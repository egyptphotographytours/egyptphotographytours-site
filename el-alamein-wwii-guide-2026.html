El Alamein 2026: A Sobering Visit to WWII Battlefields

Complete historical guide to the North Africa campaign, memorials, and photography in the desert that changed the war.

Updated March 10, 2026 El Alamein, Egypt Historical Battlefields

Introduction: Why El Alamein Still Matters in 2026

Eighty-four years after the thunder of artillery faded over the desolate ridges of El Alamein, the silent white headstones and carefully preserved bunkers continue to draw thousands of visitors each year. For the military history enthusiast, the photographer seeking haunting landscapes, or the descendant of a veteran, El Alamein is not just a site—it is a pilgrimage. In 2026, with enhanced visitor facilities and improved interpretation, the battlefield offers an even deeper connection to the events that turned the tide in North Africa during World War II.

El Alamein stands as a testament to the courage of soldiers from dozens of nations. Here, in the harsh desert between Alexandria and Mersa Matruh, the Allies under General Bernard Montgomery halted the advance of Erwin Rommel's Afrika Korps. The battles were brutal, fought in extreme temperatures, with flies, dysentery, and the constant threat of tank warfare. Today, the landscape remains largely unchanged, offering a raw and authentic window into history.

The North Africa Campaign: A Strategic Overview

To understand El Alamein, one must first grasp the broader context of the Desert War (1940-1943). The Axis powers aimed to seize the Suez Canal, cutting Britain's lifeline to its empire and oil in the Middle East. The campaign seesawed across Libya and Egypt, with fortified towns like Tobruk changing hands multiple times. By mid-1942, Rommel's forces had pushed the British Eighth Army back to a defensible line at El Alamein, just 100 kilometers from Alexandria. If the Axis broke through, Egypt and the canal would fall.

The First Battle of El Alamein (July 1942) was a desperate Allied stand that stopped Rommel's advance. Both sides were exhausted, but the line held. Then came the climactic Second Battle of El Alamein (October 23 – November 11, 1942). Montgomery, with superior numbers and a carefully planned operation (codenamed Lightfoot and Supercharge), broke through the Axis lines after twelve days of intense fighting. It was the first major land defeat for the German army in the war and a psychological turning point. Churchill famously declared: "Before Alamein we never had a victory. After Alamein we never had a defeat."

Key Facts: Second Battle of El Alamein

  • Allied Forces: 220,000 men, 1,100 tanks (including Grants and Shermans), 750 aircraft
  • Axis Forces: 115,000 men, 550 tanks (mostly Panzer III and Italian Fiat M13/40), 350 aircraft
  • Casualties: Allied: 13,500 killed/wounded; Axis: 30,000 killed/wounded and 30,000 captured
  • Commanders: Montgomery (UK) vs. Rommel (Germany)
  • Duration: October 23 – November 11, 1942

Statistical overview of El Alamein memorials and Second Battle casualties. Data sources: Commonwealth War Graves Commission, German War Graves Commission.

The Battlefields Today: Geography and Atmosphere

Driving west from Alexandria along the Mediterranean coastal road, the landscape gradually transforms from lush delta to arid, rocky desert. The railway line that was so crucial for logistics still runs parallel. The battlefield itself is a vast, open area with low ridges (Ruweisat Ridge, Miteiriya Ridge) that were key defensive positions. The desert floor is littered with fragments of war—though it is illegal to remove artifacts, you can still see remnants of makeshift defenses.

⚠️ Safety Warning: Unexploded ordnance remains a real danger in open desert areas. Visitors must stay on marked paths and follow guide instructions. Egypt Photography Tours ensures all visits are restricted to cleared areas and memorial grounds.

The silence is profound. Standing at the Commonwealth Cemetery, with row upon row of identical headstones stretching toward the Mediterranean, the scale of loss becomes visceral. The sunsets are spectacular, painting the white stone in hues of orange and purple—a poignant contrast to the violence that once occurred here.

Commonwealth War Cemetery and Memorial

Designed by Sir Hubert Worthington and maintained by the Commonwealth War Graves Commission, the El Alamein War Cemetery is the final resting place for 7,240 Commonwealth servicemen, of which 815 are unidentified. The entrance leads to a cloistered courtyard with the Stone of Remembrance and Cross of Sacrifice. The headstones, aligned with precision, bear the badges of regiments from the UK, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa, India, and other Commonwealth nations. Many graves are unidentified, marked only with the words "A Soldier of the 1939-1945 War – Known Unto God."

At the far end stands the Alamein Memorial, a sandstone colonnade unveiled by Field Marshal Montgomery himself on October 24, 1954. It commemorates 11,866 men who died with no known grave—more than 8,500 land forces and 3,000 airmen who served in campaigns across Egypt, Libya, Syria, Lebanon, Iraq, Persia, and beyond. The memorial also honors those cremated in Egypt and Libya during the war in accordance with their faith.

Photography here demands reverence. Early morning light softens the stone, while late afternoon creates long shadows that emphasize the geometry of the graves. We recommend a polarizing filter to manage glare from the white headstones.

German War Memorial

Located approximately 3 kilometers west of the Italian marker, the German memorial (built 1959) is an octagonal fortress-like structure of dark stone, reminiscent of a medieval castle. Inside, an ossuary contains the remains of 4,280 German soldiers. The interior hall is somber, with a skylight illuminating the central slab. Each side of the octagon houses an alcove with stone caskets representing German provinces, bearing plaques with the names of the dead.

Visitor Note: The memorial may be closed upon arrival; a caretaker will need to open the building. A small tip (20-50 EGP) is customary and appreciated.

Italian War Memorial

The Sacrario Militare Italiano is a striking white marble structure with a tall tower and a central altar. It houses the remains of approximately 4,800 Italian soldiers. The design is starkly beautiful, with rows of niches and a small museum. The location offers sweeping views of the battlefield and Mediterranean.

El Alamein Military Museum

Run by the Egyptian Ministry of Antiquities, the museum opened in 1965 upon a presidential decree by the late President Gamal Abd El-Nasser. It features indoor and outdoor exhibits: tanks (Sherman, Grant, Panzer IV, Italian Semovente), artillery pieces, aircraft wreckage, and personal items recovered from the battlefield. The indoor halls cover the campaign with dioramas, uniforms, and detailed maps. One hall is dedicated to each of the main combatant nations.

The museum is located at the 105-kilometer mark on the Alexandria-Matrouh road, south of the International Coastal Road. It serves as an essential first stop for context before visiting the actual battlefield sites.

Visiting El Alamein in 2026: Practical Information

Location & Access

El Alamein is approximately 130 km west of Alexandria (1.5-2 hours drive) and 280 km from Cairo (3-3.5 hours) via the northern coastal road. The Commonwealth Cemetery lies off the old coast road, slightly beyond a ridge, indicated by road signs approximately 25 meters before the low metal gates.

Best Time to Visit

October to April offers the most comfortable temperatures. Summer (May-September) is extremely hot; if visiting then, arrive early morning. The anniversary of the battle (October 23) sees commemorative services. Note that the coastal wind can be strong year-round, particularly from May-October (18-25 knots), which affects comfort but creates dramatic skies for photography.

Egypt Entry Requirements 2026

Before traveling to El Alamein, ensure you meet Egypt's entry requirements:

  • Passport: Valid for at least 6 months from arrival date with at least one blank page
  • Visa: Most travelers need a tourist visa. Options include:
    • e-Visa: Apply online at visa2egypt.gov.eg ($25 single entry, $60 multiple entry)
    • Visa on Arrival: $25 USD cash only at major airports
  • Currency: Bring USD cash for visa purchases and tips. Credit cards accepted at larger establishments but cash preferred for tips and small purchases.

Photography Tips for El Alamein

  • Golden hour: Sunrise and sunset provide warm light that adds depth to memorial architecture. The white headstones glow brilliantly at these times.
  • Composition: Use the repeating headstones as leading lines. Include the Mediterranean horizon for scale and context.
  • Equipment: Wide-angle for interiors and panoramas, telephoto for details (inscriptions, regimental badges). Polarizing filter recommended to reduce glare on white stone.
  • Ethics: Never stand on graves or touch artifacts. Maintain silence in cemetery areas. Ask permission before photographing people.
  • Drones: Strictly prohibited at all memorials without special military approval.

Visitor Tips: Respect, Safety, and Preparation

  • Dress modestly: Although a tourist site, it's respectful to cover shoulders and knees, especially in religious sections of cemeteries.
  • Sun protection: Hat, sunglasses, high-SPF sunscreen, and plenty of water (even in winter, the desert sun is intense).
  • Footwear: Comfortable walking shoes; the ground is uneven and sandy.
  • Facilities: Restrooms available at the museum. Limited facilities at memorials themselves.
  • Remember: You are walking on hallowed ground. Maintain silence in cemeteries and speak quietly at all memorials.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Why is El Alamein historically significant?

El Alamein was the site of two major battles in 1942 during WWII. The Second Battle of El Alamein marked a turning point in the North Africa campaign, halting the Axis advance towards Egypt and the Suez Canal. It was the first major land defeat for the German army under Rommel.

What memorials can I visit at El Alamein?

Visitors can explore the Commonwealth War Cemetery with 7,240 graves, the German War Memorial (an octagonal fortress-like structure housing 4,280 remains), the Italian War Memorial with approximately 4,800 remains, and the El Alamein Military Museum displaying tanks, artillery, and historical artifacts.

How do I get to El Alamein from Cairo or Alexandria?

El Alamein is approximately 130 km west of Alexandria (1.5-2 hours) and 280 km from Cairo (3-3.5 hours) via the northern coastal road. Most visitors join guided tours or hire a private car. Egypt Photography Tours offers private day trips from Alexandria or Cairo that include transport and expert guiding.

What are the opening hours for El Alamein cemeteries and museum?

The Commonwealth War Cemetery is open daily from 07:00 to 17:00. Note that visitor books are removed at 14:30 when gardeners leave, though police guard remains until closing. The Military Museum is open 9:00 AM to 4:00 PM daily. German and Italian memorials are generally accessible during daylight hours, though the German memorial may require a caretaker to open the interior.

Are there any safety concerns when visiting the battlefields?

While the main memorials are safe, unexploded ordnance may still exist in open desert areas. Visitors must stay on marked paths and follow guide instructions. Egypt Photography Tours ensures all visits are restricted to cleared areas and memorial grounds.

Can I take photographs inside the cemeteries and museum?

Photography is permitted in all memorial sites and the museum for personal use. Tripods may require permission. Our photography tours include guidance on the best light, composition, and respectful photography practices. Drones are strictly prohibited.
