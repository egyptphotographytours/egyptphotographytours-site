How to Bargain in Egypt: The Ultimate Souk Guide

Cultural etiquette, Arabic phrases, and a step-by-step negotiation masterclass for haggling with confidence in the markets of Cairo, Luxor and Aswan.

Home/ Blog/ Bargaining in the Egyptian Souk
Updated regularly 21 min read Cairo, Luxor & Aswan, Egypt By Hossam, Egyptologist & Photographer

The air is thick with the scent of spices and sandalwood. Golden lanterns cast intricate shadows on centuries-old stone walls. A merchant calls out with a smile, "Welcome, my friend — special price, just for you!" This is the Egyptian souk: part marketplace, part theater, and one of the most memorable cultural experiences anywhere in the country.

For many first-time visitors, the prospect of haggling feels intimidating. How much should you actually offer? Will you cause offense? Is there a "right" way to do this? This guide answers all of it — the etiquette that earns respect, a step-by-step negotiation framework, the Arabic phrases that change the entire interaction, and the markets worth testing your new skills in, whether you explore independently or alongside one of our guided Egypt tours.

Why Haggling Is a Cultural Cornerstone

In many Western countries, a price tag is final. In Egypt, a quoted price is the opening line of a conversation. Bargaining has been practiced in the bazaars of Cairo, Luxor and Aswan for centuries, and it isn't a battle to "win" — it's a ritual of mutual respect, wit, and connection.

Shift Your Mindset

Don't think: "How do I pay the absolute minimum?"
Do think: "What's a fair price that respects the craftsmanship and still fits my budget?" That one shift changes the entire interaction — and usually gets you a better outcome anyway.

The Haggler's Toolkit: Currency, Timing & Manner

Your success in the souk depends far less on cunning than on preparation and attitude. Three things matter most:

Cash Is King

Use Egyptian Pounds (EGP) with a mix of small denominations — vendors often can't break large notes, and exchange rates fluctuate, so check a current rate before you go.

The Power of Timing

Late afternoon and early evening are prime time — markets are lively and vendors relaxed. Never rush; a hurried buyer is quoted a higher price.

The Golden Rule: Manners

Greet first with a smile and "Salaam." Keep your tone light and humorous, never raised. Respecting a firm "no" is part of the etiquette too.

Dress & Demeanor

Modest, casual clothing without flashy jewelry helps you blend in and is less likely to invite inflated opening prices.

Your Secret Weapon: Essential Arabic Phrases

Using even a handful of Arabic words is a genuine game-changer — it signals respect and almost always leads to warmer interactions and better prices.

The Negotiation Masterclass: A Step-by-Step Guide

You've got your cash, your phrases, and your patience. Here's the dance itself, broken into six steps.

1

The Bait

Show casual interest in an item — eye contact is usually enough for the vendor to invite you to browse.

2

The Inquiry

Ask "Bikam dah?" The first number you hear is an opening offer, not a fixed price — there's no need to react with shock.

3

The First Counter

Smile, say "Ghaly awi!", and counter at roughly 30–50% of the asking price. This leaves room to negotiate toward the middle.

4

The Negotiation

Expect a counter-offer in return. Raise your price gradually, keep things light with humor, and feel free to mention prices seen elsewhere.

5

The Walk-Away

If you're stuck too high, thank them and start to leave. Vendors will often call you back with a better "final" price — and if they don't, you can always return.

6

Closing the Deal

Once you agree, seal it with "Khalas!" Renegotiating after agreeing on a price is considered poor etiquette — pay in cash and say "Shukran."

Currency Note

Exchange rates shift over time, so check a current EGP conversion before your trip rather than relying on a fixed number. A quick ATM withdrawal on arrival, with a request for smaller notes, sets you up well for the souk.

The Best Souks in Egypt to Test Your Skills

Each major Egyptian city has its own market personality. Here are the three worth prioritizing:

Khan El Khalili, Cairo

The undisputed king of Egyptian bazaars — a 14th-century labyrinth of lanterns, jewelry, spices and perfumes. Pair a visit with our Giza Pyramids guide for a full Cairo day.

Luxor Souk

Calmer than Cairo's bazaars, near Luxor Temple. Known for alabaster statues, scented oils, and high-quality Egyptian cotton.

Aswan Nubian Market

Colorful and relaxed, with friendly vendors and authentic Nubian crafts, textiles, baskets and spices.

Photographing the Souk

The souk is a paradise for photographers: the interplay of light and shadow, the explosion of color, the candid moments of daily life. A few etiquette-minded techniques go a long way.

Quick Souk Photography Tips

  • Ask first: A smile and a gesture toward your camera, or a simple "Mumkin?" (May I?), is usually met with enthusiasm.
  • Chase golden hour: Late-afternoon light through covered alleyways creates dramatic natural contrast.
  • Get close on details: Spice piles, brassware, woven textiles — texture tells the story.
  • Capture the interaction: The animated hand gestures and shared smile at the close of a deal make for the most memorable frames.
  • Respect a "no": If someone waves you off, lower the camera immediately and smile.

Avoiding Common Scams

Stay Calm, Friendly & Firm

  • The unsolicited "guide": Someone may offer to lead you to the "best shop" expecting a commission added to your price. A polite "La, shukran, I'm just looking" works well.
  • The "no change" tactic: Agree on a price before handing over money, and count change carefully.
  • The fake antiquity: Assume anything sold as an ancient artifact is a modern reproduction unless purchased through a licensed dealer with documentation.
  • The bait and switch: Check your wrapped item before leaving the stall to confirm it matches what you negotiated.

What to Buy (and What to Leave Behind)

Good Souvenirs

Hand-woven cotton and linen scarves, inlaid wooden boxes, brass and copper lanterns, papyrus art from a reputable licensed shop, spices, perfume oils, and cartouche jewelry engraved with your name in hieroglyphs.

What to Avoid

Items marketed as genuine antiquities, and anything made from coral, shell, ivory or other wildlife products — leave nature in nature, and treat "ancient" items as beautiful reproductions rather than investments.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is bargaining expected in all Egyptian shops?
No. Bargaining is expected in traditional souks such as Khan El Khalili, street stalls, and independent souvenir shops. It is not appropriate in modern shopping malls, chain stores, supermarkets, or fixed-price museum gift shops.
What is a fair opening offer when haggling in Egypt?
A respectful approach is to start at roughly 30–50% of the seller's initial asking price, leaving room to negotiate toward the middle through friendly back-and-forth.
What are some essential Arabic phrases for bargaining?
"Bikam dah?" (How much is this?), "Ghaly awi!" (Too expensive!), "Akher se'r?" (Final price?), "Khalas" (Done/deal), and "Shukran" (Thank you) cover most of the interaction.
Should I use cash or card when shopping in Egyptian souks?
Cash is strongly preferred. Bring Egyptian Pounds in small denominations, since vendors often can't break large notes, and offering cash can also help secure a better price.
How can I avoid common tourist scams in the market?
Stay calm, friendly and firm. A polite "La, shukran" handles unsolicited "guides," always agree on a final price before accepting extras, and don't hesitate to walk away from a deal that feels off.
Is bargaining considered rude in Egypt?
Not at all — in a traditional souk it's the expected and respected way of doing business. Declining to haggle at all can come across as unusual rather than polite.
What should I avoid buying in an Egyptian souk?
Avoid items sold as genuine antiquities (assume they're modern reproductions unless purchased through a licensed dealer) and anything made from coral, shell, ivory or other protected wildlife products.
Can a tour guide help me get a better price?
Yes — a local guide can advise on a realistic starting price, help translate, and create a relaxed, authentic shopping experience without the pressure of negotiating entirely on your own.
What's the best time of day to visit a souk for bargaining?
Late afternoon and early evening tend to be liveliest and most relaxed. Avoid rushing the negotiation — moving slowly and patiently typically leads to a better outcome.
Do prices differ between Khan El Khalili, Luxor, and Aswan markets?
Yes. Khan El Khalili tends to open higher given its size and popularity with tourists, Luxor's souks are more moderate, and Aswan's Nubian Market is typically the most relaxed for first-time hagglers.
