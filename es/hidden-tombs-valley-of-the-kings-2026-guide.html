The Valley of the Kings (Wadi el-Muluk) represents one of humanity's most magnificent archaeological achievements. For nearly 500 years, this remote desert valley served as the royal necropolis for pharaohs and powerful nobles of Egypt's New Kingdom. While KV62—the tomb of Tutankhamun—attracts global attention for its intact discovery, it's arguably one of theleast impressivetombs artistically and architecturally.

The real masterpieces lie in thehidden tombsthat receive only a fraction of visitors. These tombs contain vibrant paintings, intricate reliefs, complex architectural features, and historical narratives that surpass Tutankhamun's burial chamber in every artistic measure. Chapter 1: The Major Hidden Royal Tombs

𓀽 Beyond Tutankhamun: The Real Masterpieces

KV17: The Tomb of Seti IRARE ACCESS

Universally regarded as themost beautiful tomb in the Valley of the Kings. The quality of relief carving approaches perfection, with sunk relief in the outer corridors transitioning to raised relief in the inner chambers. The paint remains vibrantly preserved in many areas.

Key Features:The Astronomical Ceiling in the burial chamber, the complete Book of the Dead, and the spectacular Six-Pillared Hall. See ourValley of the Kings Guidefor more details.

KV5: The Tomb of the Sons of Ramses IIMUST-SEE

With over 150 chambers spread across multiple levels, KV5 is thelargest tomb ever constructed in Egypt. Originally thought to be a minor tomb, its true scale was only revealed in the 1990s through excavations led by Dr. Kent Weeks.

Key Features:Contains burial chambers for at least 52 sons of Ramses II. The entrance corridor provides dramatic compositional opportunities for photographers.

1

KV11: The Tomb of Ramses IIIMUST-SEE

Known as the "Harper's Tomb", Ramses III's tomb reflects both the artistic heights and the political uncertainties of his reign. It contains unusual depictions of daily life including blind harpists, Syrian bears, and exotic animals.

Photography Tip:The harpist scene in the fourth corridor is a photographic highlight. Use a fast prime lens (f/1.8 or wider) since flash photography is prohibited. See ourfresco photography guidefor more techniques.

2

KV8: The Tomb of Merenptah

The second largest tomb in the valley, featuring a spectacular quartzite sarcophagus and extensive corridor decorations. The deep descent and massive scale make it a favorite for those seeking dramatic architectural photography.

3

KV14: The Tomb of Tausret and Setnakht

A unique double tomb originally built for a queen who eventually ruled as pharaoh (Tausret), then usurped and expanded by Setnakht. The architectural modifications provide a fascinating glimpse into the political turmoil of the late 19th Dynasty.

Chapter 2: The Lesser-Known Gems

KV35: The Tomb of Amenhotep II and Royal Cache

While Amenhotep II's tomb itself is impressive, its greatest significance came in 1898 when Victor Loret discovered it contained aroyal mummy cachewith nine pharaohs including Thutmose IV, Amenhotep III, and Seti II. The bodies had been moved there by priests during the 21st Dynasty to protect them from tomb robbers. The intact sarcophagus remains in situ, unlike most royal tombs. For more on Luxor's West Bank, read ourLuxor East & West Bank Guide.

KV55: The Amarna Period Mystery Tomb

Discovered in 1907, KV55 contained a jumble of Amarna period materials, leading to a century of debate about its occupant. The tomb contained parts of a gilded shrine made for Queen Tiye, canopic jars, and a badly deteriorated mummy. Theories suggest it may contain Akhenaten or Smenkhkare. The tomb's importance lies in its connection to theAmarna period, Egypt's dramatic religious revolution. The artistic style differs markedly from other Valley tombs, showing the distinctive elongated figures of Amarna art.

Chapter 3: Photography Guide for Tomb Interiors

Low-Light Mastery
Use a fast lens (f/1.4-f/2.8), full-frame camera for better high ISO performance, and shoot in RAW. ISO 1600-6400 is standard.
Available Light
Modern tombs have carefully designed artificial lighting. Learn to work with this light rather than fighting it. Position yourself to maximize texture.
Color Accuracy
Different tombs use different lighting temperatures. Use custom white balance settings or a color checker card for each tomb.
No Flash Policy
Flash photography is universally prohibited to protect fragile pigments. Respect the rules to help preserve these masterpieces for future generations.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are the most overlooked tombs in the Valley of the Kings?
The most overlooked tombs include KV5 (Sons of Ramses II), KV17 (Seti I), KV11 (Ramses III), KV8 (Merenptah), KV14 (Tausret and Setnakht), KV47 (Siptah), KV23 (Ay), KV57 (Horemheb), KV43 (Thutmose IV), KV35 (Amenhotep II), and KV16 (Ramses I). These contain extraordinary artwork and architecture but receive fewer visitors than Tutankhamun's tomb.
Can I visit all the tombs in the Valley of the Kings?
No, only about 8-12 tombs are typically open to the public at any given time due to conservation efforts. The standard ticket allows access to 3 tombs (excluding Tutankhamun and Seti I which require separate tickets). Our exclusive photography tours can arrange special access to tombs that are normally closed or have restricted access periods.
What is the best time of day to photograph tombs in the Valley of the Kings?
Early morning (7:30-9:30 AM) is ideal as the sun illuminates the valley beautifully and crowds are minimal. Late afternoon (3:00-5:00 PM) also offers good light and fewer visitors. For interior tomb photography, specialized low-light techniques are required regardless of time of day. Our photography tours include instruction on capturing tomb interiors without flash.
Which tomb has the best preserved artwork?
KV17 (Seti I) contains the most exquisitely preserved artwork with vibrant colors intact. KV11 (Ramses III) also has exceptionally preserved reliefs showing daily life and religious scenes. KV62 (Tutankhamun) while famous has simpler decoration compared to these larger pharaonic tombs. Our guides prioritize these well-preserved tombs for photography enthusiasts.
Do Egypt Photography Tours offer special access to restricted tombs?
Yes, through our exclusive partnerships and early morning access arrangements, we can provide entry to tombs that are typically closed to the general public or have limited opening hours.
