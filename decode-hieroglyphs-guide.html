The Basics of Hieroglyphs

The word "hieroglyph" comes from Greek: hieros (sacred) + glypho (carvings). But ancient Egyptians called their writing "mdw nṯr" (medu netcher) – "the gods' words." This reveals the sacred nature of this script that endured for over 3,500 years.

The Three Types of Hieroglyphic Signs

To decode hieroglyphs, you must understand that they are not just "picture writing." They function through three distinct types of signs:

The Three Types of Hieroglyphic Signs
Sign TypeFunctionExamplesUsage
PhonogramsRepresent sounds (like our alphabet)𓄤 (nfr = "good"), 𓂋 (r = "mouth")~45% of texts
LogogramsRepresent whole words/concepts𓁹 (ir = "eye"), 𓉐 (pr = "house")~35% of texts
DeterminativesClarify word meanings (no sound)𓀀 (man), 𓇋 (god)~20% of texts

The Hieroglyph Alphabet: Your Decoding Toolkit

Critical Understanding: Hieroglyphs represent consonants only (no vowels). This is why Egyptologists add "e" between consonants when pronouncing words: "nfr" becomes "nefer."

The 24 Uniliteral Signs (Single-Consonant Hieroglyphs)

These are your foundation—the closest thing to an "alphabet" in ancient Egyptian. Master these, and you can sound out almost any word:

  • 𓇋 (i): Reed - Often begins words.
  • 𓂋 (r): Mouth - Extremely common.
  • 𓅓 (m): Owl - Very common.
  • 𓈖 (n): Water ripple - Ubiquitous.
  • 𓏏 (t): Loaf of bread - Feminine ending.
  • 𓄡 (ḥ): Twisted flax - In many titles.
  • 𓎛 (ḥ): Twisted wick - Religious contexts.
  • 𓃀 (b): Foot - Common words.
  • 𓊪 (p): Stool - Names, titles.
  • 𓆑 (f): Horned viper - Verbs, names.

For a deeper dive into the history of these symbols, explore our guide on the Grand Egyptian Museum, where many of the original artifacts displaying these signs are housed.

Mastering Reading Direction

This is the #1 challenge for beginners. Hieroglyphs can be written left-to-right, right-to-left, or top-to-bottom. Follow these clues:

  • Animal/human figures face the start: If birds face right, read right-to-left.
  • Asymmetric signs indicate direction: The mouth hieroglyph (𓂋) opens toward the beginning of the line.
  • Columns read top-to-bottom: Then determine left-right direction within the column.

Photography Pro Tip

When photographing hieroglyphs for later decoding: Include the entire inscription boundary in your frame, note which way animal/human figures face, and photograph from straight ahead to avoid distortion. For more tips on capturing temple details, see our fresco and tomb photography guide.

Grammar & Royal Names

You don't need to become a grammar expert, but understanding these basics will dramatically improve your decoding:

  • 𓏏 (t): Feminine ending (appears after feminine nouns).
  • 𓍯𓍯 (w): Plural marker (two quail chicks = plural).
  • 𓀀 (determinative): Indicates person/man.

Royal Titulary (Pharaoh's Names)

Every pharaoh had five names. Learn to recognize them in cartouches (oval frames):

  • Horus Name: Protective falcon (𓅃).
  • Throne Name: "He of the sedge and bee" (𓆥).
  • Birth Name: "Son of Ra" + personal name (often seen in cartouches).

You'll see these names repeatedly at sites like Karnak and Luxor Temples and the Temple of Hatshepsut.

Photography Techniques for Inscriptions

Capturing inscriptions for later study requires specific techniques, especially in the dim light of tombs or the harsh glare of outdoor temples.

  • Gear: Wide-angle (16-35mm) for walls, Macro (90-100mm) for details, Polarizing filter for glare.
  • Lighting: Side lighting reveals carving depth. Use an LED panel if permitted.
  • Settings: High ISO for tombs, polarizer for outdoor temples.

Photography Restrictions

Always check the latest photography permits before your trip. Tripods are often restricted, and flash is prohibited in many tombs to protect the pigments. Our guides know exactly where and when you can shoot.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it possible for tourists to learn to read hieroglyphs before visiting Egypt?
Absolutely! With focused study, tourists can learn basic hieroglyph decoding in 2-3 weeks. Our Egypt Photography Tours include pre-trip learning materials and on-site decoding practice with certified Egyptologists. Most travelers can learn to read simple inscriptions, names, and common phrases before their trip.
What are the 3 types of hieroglyphic signs?
Egyptian hieroglyphs use three sign types: 1) Phonograms (represent sounds, like an alphabet), 2) Logograms (represent whole words/concepts), and 3) Determinatives (clarify word meanings without making a sound).
How do you know which direction to read hieroglyphs?
Hieroglyphs can be written left-to-right, right-to-left, or top-to-bottom. The key is to look at the direction the animals and human figures are facing. They always face the beginning of the line. If a bird faces right, you read from right to left.
Can I practice decoding real hieroglyphs during Egypt Photography Tours?
Yes! All our tours include hands-on hieroglyph decoding sessions at archaeological sites. Our Private Pyramids Tour features Giza tomb inscription decoding, and the 7-Day Discovery Tour includes temple wall reading practice at Luxor and Karnak.
What's the most common mistake beginners make when reading hieroglyphs?
The biggest mistake is reading direction incorrectly. Beginners often assume all text reads left-to-right like English. Remember to always check the direction the figures are facing to determine the start of the line.
