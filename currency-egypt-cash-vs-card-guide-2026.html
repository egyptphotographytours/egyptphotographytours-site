Chapter 1: The Egyptian Pound (EGP) - Your Essential Currency

The official currency of Egypt is the Egyptian Pound, often abbreviated as EGP or symbolized by "LE" (livre égyptienne). One pound is divided into 100 piastres. Understanding the notes and coins is your first step to financial fluency. If you are planning a budget trip, understanding the EGP is essential for our Egypt on a Budget Guide.

💵 Banknotes and Coins: A Visual Guide

The Denominations

You'll encounter banknotes in denominations of 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, and 200 EGP. Coins exist for 25 and 50 piastres, and 1 pound, but small paper notes are more common for tipping and small purchases.

The Lookalike Trap

A common pitfall for tourists is confusing the brownish 50 EGP note with the greenish 5 EGP note, or the orange 100 EGP note with the yellowish 10 EGP note. When paying, state clearly, "This is 50 pounds," and count your change carefully. This simple habit prevents misunderstandings.

The Dual-Currency Reality: EGP and USD

While the Egyptian Pound is king, the US Dollar operates as a strong secondary currency, especially in tourism. Many tour packages, Nile cruises, and upscale hotels quote prices in dollars. You can often pay for your visa on arrival with USD, and it's the preferred currency for larger tips. Euros and British Pounds are also widely accepted at exchange offices. For more on arriving smoothly, check our Airport Accessibility Guide.

Insider Tip: The "Clean Bill" Rule

Egyptians are particular about the condition of foreign banknotes. Bring crisp, new, and undamaged US dollars or Euros. Notes with tears, stamps, or excessive creasing are frequently rejected by banks and exchange shops.

Chapter 2: Cash vs. Card - The 2026 Verdict

Can you survive on plastic alone in Egypt? The short answer is no. Egypt remains a predominantly cash-based society, but the role of cards is growing. Here's your definitive breakdown of where each reigns supreme.

Chapter 3: Navigating Credit Card Usage in 2026

The landscape for card usage has shifted in 2026, primarily due to measures by the Central Bank of Egypt (CBE) to manage foreign currency reserves. Here's what you, as a traveler, need to know. For a deep dive into which cards work best, read our Egypt Travel Credit Cards Guide.

1

New CBE Regulations Local Cards

If you're using a card issued by an Egyptian bank, you must notify your bank of your travel dates to activate higher spending limits abroad. Limits have also been tightened to combat currency speculation. However, this primarily affects local cards.

2

For International Travelers Foreign Cards

These CBE regulations primarily target cards issued within Egypt. For tourists using cards from the US, Europe, or Asia, the impact is minimal. Your foreign-issued Visa or Mastercard should work at most ATMs and point-of-sale terminals in hotels and major businesses.

3

The Golden Rule: Always Have Cash Backup

Even with a working card, you might occasionally encounter a machine that has trouble with international chips, or a network outage. Always have a backup card and a cash reserve. Never rely solely on plastic.

Chapter 4: The Art of Getting Cash - Exchange & ATM Mastery

Where and how you get your Egyptian pounds can save or cost you a significant amount of money. Let's rank your options from best to worst. To avoid common pitfalls, check our Scams in Egypt Guide.

1. ATMs (The Gold Standard)

Best for: Getting EGP at the official exchange rate with low fees.
Tip: Use ATMs from major banks like Banque Misr, CIB, or HSBC. Always decline the "dynamic currency conversion" (DCC) offered by the machine.

2. Official Exchange Offices

Best for: Exchanging USD or EUR cash in city centers.
Tip: Rates here are often slightly better than banks. Look for licensed shops with electronic boards. Count your money carefully.

3. Banks (Reliable but Slow)

Best for: Large, official transactions where security is paramount.
Tip: Banks have limited hours (8:30 AM - 2:00 PM, closed Friday/Saturday). You may need your passport.

4. Airport Desks (Avoid)

Best for: Absolute emergencies only.
Tip: They offer the worst rates, sometimes 3-5% worse than city rates. Only exchange a tiny amount for immediate needs like a taxi fare.

Chapter 5: Tipping (Baksheesh) - The Right Currency Matters

Tipping is a deeply ingrained part of Egyptian culture and is expected for many services. Using the correct currency shows respect and understanding. For a complete guide on cultural etiquette, see our Dress Code Guide and Religious Site Etiquette Guide.

Currency Strategy for Tipping

  • Small Tips (5-50 EGP): Use small Egyptian Pound notes. Keep a stash of 5s, 10s, and 20s in a separate pocket for bathroom attendants, hotel bellhops, and quick favors.
  • Large Tips (50-500+ EGP): For your dedicated tour guide, private driver, or the collective Nile cruise tip, clean US dollars are often preferred and more appreciated.

Budgeting Tip: A practical way to budget for personal expenses and tips is to allocate 10-15% of your total tour cost for discretionary spending and baksheesh.

Chapter 7: Keeping Your Money Safe

Financial safety is paramount. Whether you are a solo female traveler or traveling with family, protecting your funds is essential. For specific safety advice, read our Solo Female Safety Report and Family Safety Guide.

Do's and Don'ts of Money Safety

  • DO use the hotel safe for your passport and excess cash.
  • DO carry a decoy wallet with a small amount of cash for minor purchases, keeping your main funds hidden in a money belt.
  • DO notify your bank and credit card companies of your travel dates to prevent them from freezing your card.
  • DON'T flash large wads of cash in public.
  • DON'T let street vendors or unofficial guides handle your money or credit card. Keep it in your sight at all times.
  • DON'T rely on a single payment method. Always have at least one backup card and a cash reserve.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the official currency of Egypt?
The official currency is the Egyptian Pound (EGP), often symbolized as LE (livre égyptienne). One pound is divided into 100 piastres. Banknotes come in denominations of 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, and 200 EGP.
Can I use my credit card everywhere in Egypt in 2026?
No, Egypt remains a cash-heavy society. Credit cards (Visa/Mastercard) are widely accepted in upscale hotels, fine-dining restaurants, and for booking tours. However, you will need cash for traditional markets (souks), street food, small shops, taxis, and all baksheesh (tipping).
Should I exchange money before I travel to Egypt?
It is generally not recommended to exchange significant amounts before arriving, as rates outside Egypt are often poor. Bring a mix of clean, new US dollars or Euros in cash for emergencies and withdraw Egyptian pounds from ATMs upon arrival for the best rates.
What is the best currency to bring to Egypt?
The US Dollar is the most popular and easily exchanged foreign currency, followed by the Euro and British Pound. Bring crisp, new, undamaged bills, as torn or marked notes are often rejected. You can use USD to pay for visas on arrival.
Do I need to tip in Egypt and what currency should I use?
Tipping, or 'baksheesh', is expected for many services. For small tips (bathroom attendants, bellhops), use small Egyptian Pound notes (5, 10, 20 EGP). For larger tips (tour guides, drivers), clean US dollars are often preferred.
